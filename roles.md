# Role Reference

Per-role detail used to populate briefing cards in Step 4 of [`SKILL.md`](SKILL.md).

> Based on Alain Cardon, MCC — *Systemic Team Coaching Delegated Processes*, Metasystème Coaching.
> <https://www.metasysteme-coaching.eu/english/systemic-team-coaching-delegated-processes/>

Each role section uses the same four-block structure so the briefing-card template can read it mechanically: **Mission · Before · During · Avoid · Hand-off**.

---

## Facilitator (Moderator)

**Mission.** Manage the team's collective energy and participation across the meeting, like an orchestra conductor.

**Before the meeting**
- Re-read the agenda and identify the two or three sequences likely to drift or escalate.
- Decide where you will physically stand or position your camera so you face the group.
- Agree a quick signalling convention with the Pacer and the Decision Driver (e.g. a hand sign).

**During the meeting**
- Stand or sit forward; face the group rather than being submerged in it.
- Open and close each agenda sequence explicitly: "we are now starting sequence X for Y minutes".
- Use eye contact, gesture, and short prompts to bring quiet attendees in and to keep over-talkers from monopolising airtime.
- Treat the Pacer's and Decision Driver's interventions as helpful — act on them visibly.
- Add your own ideas occasionally, but never enough to become the main speaker.

**Avoid**
- Hogging the discussion or turning the meeting into your monologue.
- Ignoring Pacer / Decision Driver signals.
- Letting one or two attendees dominate while others go silent.

**Hand-off**
- Close cleanly: confirm the next sequence or end the meeting; cue the Process Coach for the final 10 minutes.

## Decision Driver

**Mission.** Provoke decisions, then record them — clearly, measurably, with a pilot and a deadline.

**Before the meeting**
- Open the decisions template (see [`templates/minutes.md`](templates/minutes.md)) ready to type during the meeting.
- Skim the agenda to spot which sequences are decision-bearing and which are merely informational.

**During the meeting**
- Regularly ask interventions like: "Are we close to a decision?", "How would we phrase that as a decision?", "We disagree — so what decision can we still make now?".
- For each decision, capture in writing: **what** is decided, **who** pilots it (exactly one person), **by when** (a specific date), and **how** success will be measured.
- Read the wording back to the group before moving on.
- Send the decisions list to all attendees immediately after the meeting (do not wait until "later").

**Avoid**
- Allowing two pilots on the same decision — it dissolves accountability.
- Vague or undated deadlines ("soon", "next quarter", "ASAP").
- Letting the group change subjects without first naming what was decided on the previous one.

**Hand-off**
- Distribute the filled decisions table to all attendees before they leave the room / call.
- Pass the file to whoever owns the minutes (often you).

## Pacer

**Mission.** Make the passage of time inside each short sequence audible to the whole team. You are a clock, not a cop.

**Before the meeting**
- Note each agenda sequence and its allotted duration. Sequences should be **20 minutes or less**.
- Decide your signalling style (verbal cue, chat message, physical card).

**During the meeting**
- Announce time spent / time remaining roughly **every 5 minutes** of every sequence. Example: "10 minutes used out of 20".
- If the team runs over, keep announcing every 5 minutes ("we are now 5 minutes overtime on the 20-minute contract"). Do not press them to stop.
- Stay neutral — your job is to make time visible, not to enforce it.

**Avoid**
- Acting as a time cop ("hurry up!", "stop now!") — that is the team's culture issue, not yours.
- Deciding unilaterally to extend or shrink a sequence — escalate that to the Facilitator.
- Going silent because "they know what time it is".

**Hand-off**
- At the end, share a short observation with the Process Coach about which sequences blew their budget — useful input for the feed-forward round.

---

## Process Coach

**Mission.** In the **final 10 minutes**, give each attendee one piece of individual, future-oriented, solution-focused feed-forward.

**Before the meeting**
- Reserve the last 10 minutes on the agenda — non-negotiable.
- Bring a small notebook or open a notes file to jot one observation per attendee during the meeting.

**During the meeting**
- Listen and watch. For each attendee, capture **one** specific behaviour you would invite them to try differently next time.
- Phrase coaching notes as future actions, not past judgements.

**Final 10 minutes — delivery**
- Address each attendee by name, looking at them directly.
- Phrase each piece as a forward-looking suggestion: "Next meeting, I suggest you try ...", "I'm not sure this will land, but you could experiment with ...".
- Keep it specific and individual.

**Avoid**
- Collective statements ("the energy was low", "we have a listening problem") — they feel unjust to those who don't fit.
- Past-tense finger-pointing ("you interrupted three times in the first sequence") — it generates resentment, not change.
- Skipping the round because "we ran out of time" — protect those 10 minutes.

**Hand-off**
- Drop a short anonymised summary into the minutes' "process notes" section so the team can track patterns over time.

## Optional roles

### Host
Used when meetings rotate locations. Owns the logistics for the meeting venue, including any visits, side-meetings or local feedback session that gives the host site value from the visit.

### Technician
Used when meetings rely on complex AV, hybrid setups, or specialised tools. Tests the kit beforehand, owns connectivity, and recovers fast when something fails — so the meeting itself is never derailed by tooling.

### Scribe
Used **only** if the Facilitator can't comfortably write on the board / shared doc. The Scribe writes only what the Facilitator dictates and stays out of decision wording — that belongs to the Decision Driver.

---

## The decision-maker (not a rotating role)

The team leader / boss validates decisions and remains accountable for them, but is **not** assigned any of the rotating roles above. A healthy delegated meeting can run even when the decision-maker is absent, provided they are informed of every decision made.
